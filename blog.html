Goal Setting

Your Best Year Yet: A Practical Guide to Goals That Actually Stick

🕑 8 min read 📅 April 2026 🆕 Free planner inside
Blog Post

Every January, millions of people write down their goals. By February, most have abandoned them. Not because the goals were wrong, but because the system behind them was.

This isn't another "set SMART goals" post. This is about building a structure that actually works when motivation runs dry, which it will, and life gets complicated, which it does.

The problem isn't that you don't know what you want. The problem is you haven't built an environment that makes it easy to become the person who does those things consistently.

1. Start with your values, not your goals

Most people start goal-setting by asking "what do I want this year?" That's the wrong first question. Goals change. Life changes. But your values, what genuinely matters to you, stay far more stable.

Before writing a single goal, answer these three questions honestly:

1

What do I want more of in my life?

Think in broad categories: learning, freedom, connection, impact, stability, creativity. Don't filter yourself here.

2

What do I want less of?

Stress, wasted time, financial worry, comparison, or situations that drain you. These tell you just as much about your values as the positives do.

3

What would I regret not pursuing?

Regret is a powerful signal. It points directly at the things that matter most to you, the things that feel "safe to skip" now but won't feel safe later.

From your answers, identify your top 3 values for this season of life. These become your filter for every goal you set. If a goal doesn't connect to at least one value, cut it.

2. Turn values into 90-day targets

Annual goals are too far away to feel real. 90 days is short enough to feel urgent, long enough to see real change. For each of your 3 values, define one clear target, something you could achieve in the next 12 weeks.

A good 90-day target is:

  • Specific enough that you'd know if you achieved it
  • Ambitious enough that you'd be proud of it
  • Within your control, not dependent on other people's decisions

Example: Instead of "get healthier" (vague, annual), try "complete 3 workouts per week for 12 consecutive weeks" (specific, 90-day, within your control).

3. Break targets into weekly actions

A 90-day target is still too abstract to act on today. The bridge between your target and your daily life is the weekly action, the specific thing you'll do each week that moves you closer.

Every Sunday (or whenever your week resets), identify 3 to 5 weekly actions tied to your 90-day targets. Write them down somewhere visible, not buried in an app. Physical visibility matters.

A weekly action isn't a to-do item. It's a recurring commitment: "I will do X, Y times, on these days." The schedule matters as much as the task itself.

4. Build the habit - not the outcome

Here's the part most goal advice skips: you can't control outcomes. You can control behaviour. A student aiming to graduate with first-class honours can't control the exam results, but they can control how many hours they study, how many past papers they complete, and how consistently they show up.

Shift your focus from outcomes to habits. Ask yourself: what does the person who achieves this goal do consistently? Then start doing those things, before you feel ready, before you see results, before it feels natural.

It won't feel natural for a while. That's completely normal. The feeling follows the action, not the other way around.

5. The weekly review that keeps it all alive

Everything above falls apart without a weekly review. This is the single habit that separates people who achieve their goals from people who set them and forget them.

Your weekly review doesn't need to be long. Set aside 15 minutes at the same time every week and answer four questions:

1

What did I complete last week?

Celebrate wins, however small. Progress compounds.

2

What did I miss, and why?

No judgment, just diagnosis. If you missed the same thing twice, the system needs to change, not your willpower.

3

What's the most important thing for next week?

Pick one thing that, if done, would make next week a success regardless of everything else.

4

Am I still aligned with my values?

Occasionally your goals need adjusting. That's not failure, it's growth. Your values should remain stable; the targets and actions can flex.

That's it. No 2-hour planning session. No complex system. Just 15 minutes of honest reflection and intentional planning, done consistently every week.

The gap between where you are and where you want to be isn't talent. It isn't luck. It's the accumulation of small, consistent actions that you take when no one is watching and nothing feels urgent.
